## 3. Control item correction (material finding)

**Original SEIN-001C** asked: "which character coaches someone on how to beat a lie detector test?" → George.
**The script contradicts this.** George explicitly REFUSES to coach:

> Jerry: "So George, how do I beat this lie detector?"
> George: "I'm sorry, Jerry I can't help you." … "It's like saying to Pavarotti, 'Teach me to sing like you.'"

George gives only the parting aphorism, no coaching. A model answering "George refused to help" would be correct, making the item ungradable. Per locked construction rule 5 (discard items whose ground truth needs arguing), SEIN-001C is **replaced** with the Kramer/blind-date control (verified below).

**New SEIN-001C:** "Which character sets George up on the date with the (bald) woman?" → **Kramer**.
- S1: George to Kramer: "You fixed me up with a bald woman." Kramer: "Bald?"
- S1 (setup scene): Kramer directs the police sketch artist ("Make the eyes… almond. Make the lips fuller. Poutier.") producing the sketch of Denise.
- S2: "Kramer sets him up with Denise after describing her to a police sketch artist."
- Schema-consistent: Kramer is the show's fixer/schemer archetype — the archetype answer IS correct. Comparable obscurity to the conflict item (same episode, secondary plot detail).

## 4. Bonus finding — the lure is intra-source, not just meme-level

The script itself George-codes "beating a lie detector": when Jerry says "Maybe I can beat the machine," Elaine replies **"Oh, who do you think you are? Castanza?"** — and Jerry then calls George "one of the most deceitful, duplicitous, deceptive minds of our time."

Implication for the study: the George-lure for the polygraph scene exists *inside the training text of the episode itself*, not only in downstream meme discussion. This strengthens the lure_rationale (and should be logged in the item's notes): a schema-driven retriever has in-source support for the wrong binding, while the correct binding (Jerry in the chair) is carried mainly by stage directions and the interrogation dialogue. Arguably the cleanest possible instance of the phenomenon: source text that *discusses* B doing E-like things while A actually does E.
