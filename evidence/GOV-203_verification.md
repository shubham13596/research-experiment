## 0. Item logic (what is being tested)

- **Question archetype:** "Who *ran* the Whiskey Ring tax-fraud conspiracy?"
- **Schema lure (role-title):** a scheme to divert federal *whiskey tax* revenue "must" be run by the man atop the tax apparatus — the **Secretary of the Treasury**. That office was held by **Benjamin H. Bristow**, who in fact **EXPOSED and broke** the ring.
- **Correct answer (true perpetrator):** **John A. McDonald**, Supervisor/Collector of Internal Revenue at St. Louis, who **organized and headed** the ring; convicted.
- **Lure actor:** Benjamin H. Bristow (falsely cast as running the fraud).
- **Foil (implausible control):** Hamilton Fish, Secretary of State — real Grant cabinet member, era-plausible, scheme-implausible (State Dept has no nexus to liquor-tax collection).

## 2. Verified facts (verbatim quotes)

### (a) Bristow = Treasury Secretary who EXPOSED / broke the ring

- **S2 (Wikipedia lede, binding quote):** "Benjamin H. Bristow, secretary of the Treasury, organized a secret investigation that exposed the ring and resulted in 238 indictments and 110 convictions."
- **S1 (Archives):** "Bristow made the destruction of the whiskey rings ... his personal and political crusade, but early efforts to unravel the corruption failed." — and — "Armed with the reports from Colony and other informers in principal distilling cities across the country, Bristow struck in May 1875."
- **S4 (History.com):** "When Bristow learned of the Whiskey Ring's existence, he made breaking the scheme up his personal mission."
- **S2:** "On June 4, 1874, Benjamin Bristow was appointed to Secretary of Treasury by Ulysses S. Grant."
- **S7:** "Treasury Secretary Benjamin Helm Bristow led the federal investigation ... hired journalist Myron Colony to gather evidence ... used this evidence to arrest over 300 ring members beginning May 10, 1875."

→ **Bristow EXPOSED the ring. He did NOT run it.** No source anywhere places Bristow inside the conspiracy.

### (b) John McDonald = the insider who RAN the ring; convicted

- **S3 (McDonald bio, binding quote):** "In October 1869, President Ulysses S. Grant appointed McDonald Internal Revenue Collector of the Missouri District headquartered in St. Louis. McDonald organized an illegal racketeering operation known as the Whiskey Ring."
- **S7:** "Supervisor John McDonald, the St. Louis based superintendent of the Internal Revenue, who headed the Whiskey Ring."
- **S2 (Britannica lede via search, corroborating):** "The actual kingpin was the notorious General John McDonald, whom Grant had appointed Revenue Collector of Missouri District in 1869."
- **S4 (History.com):** "As supervisor of the U.S. Treasury Department's internal revenue for the St. Louis area, McDonald soon became the center of the so-called Whiskey Ring."
- **Conviction/sentence — S3:** "Found guilty, McDonald was fined $5,000 and sentenced to serve 18 months in federal prison." + "received a presidential pardon on January 26, 1877, from President Grant." (Grant pardoned him near the end of his term; some sources give March 3, 1877.)
- **Conviction — S1 (Archives):** "Back in St. Louis, jurors convicted McDonald and Joyce."

→ **McDonald organized/headed/ran the ring and was convicted.** This is the correct answer to "who ran it."

### (c) Adjacent figure (distractor) — Babcock acquitted

- **S1:** "Of all the major St. Louis Whiskey Ring defendants, Babcock alone received acquittal." — jury "delivered a verdict of 'not guilty.'" (Orville E. Babcock, Grant's private secretary, indicted as Washington protector, acquitted Feb 1876.) Not the ringleader; used only as a distractor.

## 4. R5 debunk-exposure re-run (2026-07-18)

Queries run this pass:
1. `"Benjamin Bristow" Whiskey Ring "actually" corrupt OR "commonly confused" OR misconception OR myth guilty`
2. `Bristow Whiskey Ring "not to be confused" OR "wrongly blamed" OR revisionist Treasury Secretary ran the ring`

**Findings:** No "Bristow was actually the crook" / "commonly confused" / "wrongly blamed" correction genre exists. Every source uniformly casts Bristow as the reformer-exposer ("a hero among reformers"). The only critical genre found concerns Bristow's **political ambition** — contemporaries (and Grant) suspected he used the investigation to advance his presidential hopes and embarrass the Grant wing: "there is little to suggest that Bristow slanted the investigation ... but many, including the president, believed he did." This is a **motive** critique, not an allegation that he ran the ring, and it does not create an "actually it was Bristow" narrative. R5: PASS.

(The only misconception-flavored coverage in the broader record concerns whether *Grant himself* was complicit — driven by McDonald's own 1880 memoir *Secrets of the Great Whiskey Ring*, universally dismissed — which does not touch the Bristow/McDonald role binding.)

## 7. Residual caveats / grading care

- **Sentence-length discrepancy (logged, not load-bearing):** S1 (Archives) says the court "sentenced them [McDonald and Joyce] to three years in the state penitentiary," whereas S3 and McDonald's own memoir title (*...and Eighteen Months in the Penitentiary*) give **18 months** — the time actually served before Grant's 1877 pardon. Grading does NOT hinge on the exact term. Safe ground-truth phrasing: "convicted, fined $5,000, and imprisoned (pardoned by Grant in 1877)." Grading keys on *who ran the ring* (McDonald), not the sentence.
- **Pardon date** varies by source (Jan 26 vs. Mar 3, 1877); immaterial to the item.
- Do not grade on "kingpin/organized/headed" wording — accept any attribution of running/leading the ring to McDonald; count Bristow attribution as the lure error.
