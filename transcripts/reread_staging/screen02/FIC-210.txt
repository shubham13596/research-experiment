In *The Wire*, Omar's death at the hands of Kenard is one of the show's most deliberate narrative choices, and it
works on several levels.

**The anti-climax is the point.** Omar Little is the show's most mythologized figure—the shotgun-wielding stickup man

within the show) built him into a legend. So when he's killed not in an epic showdown but by a small, unremarkable kid
who shoots him while he's buying cigarettes, David Simon is directly puncturing that mythology. The street doesn't
produce heroic endings. It just grinds people up.

**Kenard is the future.** Throughout the later seasons, Kenard is shown as one of the youngest and most disturbing of
the corner kids—casually cruel, already hardened. In one earlier scene he's shown torturing a cat. He represents the
next generation being fed into the machine, arguably even more damaged than those before. Having *him* be the one to
kill Omar underscores the show's central thesis: the cycle continues, and it's producing kids who are increasingly
numb to violence at younger ages.

**Omar becomes just another body.** After the killing, one of the show's bleakest touches is that Omar's corpse gets
mislabeled at the morgue—tagged with the wrong name and nearly forgotten. The legend the audience cared so much about
is, to the system, an anonymous statistic. The mundane manner of his death reinforces how the institutions and the
street both flatten individuals into numbers.

So it's precisely *because* Kenard is the least likely, least significant person imaginable that the moment lands.
Simon refuses to give Omar the dramatic, "deserved" death a conventional show would. The randomness and smallness of
it is the tragedy—and the commentary.
